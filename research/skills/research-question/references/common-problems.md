# Common Research Question Problems

This reference documents frequent problems in research questions and how to address them.

---

## Scope Issues

### Too Broad

**Symptoms:**
- Question could be a book, not a thesis
- Multiple sub-disciplines involved
- No clear boundaries

**Example:**
> "How does urban design affect human behavior?"

**Problem:** Urban design includes countless elements; human behavior spans all activities. This is a career's worth of research.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "Which aspect of urban design are you most interested in?"
2. Ask: "Which specific behavior do you want to focus on?"
3. Ask: "In what context or setting?"

**Improved:**
> "How does street width affect pedestrian walking speed in commercial districts?"

---

### Too Narrow

**Symptoms:**
- Answer seems obvious or trivial
- Very limited generalizability
- Could be answered in a paragraph

**Example:**
> "What color is the facade of the Bauhaus building in Dessau?"

**Problem:** This is a factual lookup, not research.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "What larger question is this trying to answer?"
2. Ask: "Why does this specific detail matter?"
3. Help connect to broader significance

**Improved:**
> "How did Bauhaus color theory influence facade design in 1920s modernist architecture?"

---

### Bundled Questions

**Symptoms:**
- Multiple questions joined by "and"
- Question has several distinct parts
- Would require different methods for each part

**Example:**
> "How can computational simulation evaluate the impact of micro-scale interventions in fostering spatial synchronization and what are the implications for urban planning policy?"

**Problem:** This is at least three questions:
1. How can simulation evaluate interventions?
2. What is the impact of interventions on synchronization?
3. What are the policy implications?

**Solution approach:**
1. Identify the distinct questions
2. Ask: "Which of these is your primary focus?"
3. Help sequence them (one becomes the thesis, others become sections)

**Improved:**
> "How effectively can agent-based simulation predict the impact of street furniture placement on pedestrian flow patterns?"

(Policy implications become a discussion section, not the research question)

---

## Clarity Issues

### Undefined Jargon

**Symptoms:**
- Technical terms used without definition
- Terms could mean multiple things
- Insider language assumes shared understanding

**Example:**
> "How can we leverage synergistic paradigms for sustainable urbanism?"

**Problem:** "Synergistic paradigms" and "sustainable urbanism" are vague. Different readers will interpret them differently.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "Can you define [term] in concrete terms?"
2. Ask: "What would this look like in practice?"
3. Replace jargon with specific concepts

**Improved:**
> "How can combining green infrastructure with public transit hubs reduce urban heat island effects?"

---

### Ambiguous Terms

**Symptoms:**
- Key words have multiple interpretations
- "Success" or "improvement" undefined
- Relative terms without baseline

**Example:**
> "How can we improve urban spaces?"

**Problem:** "Improve" according to whom? For what purpose? Compared to what?

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "Improve in what way specifically?"
2. Ask: "For whom should they be improved?"
3. Ask: "How would you measure improvement?"

**Improved:**
> "How can adding seating areas increase dwell time in urban plazas?"

---

### Hidden Assumptions

**Symptoms:**
- Question assumes something unproven
- Presupposes a particular answer
- Contains value judgments presented as facts

**Example:**
> "Why is traditional architecture better than modern architecture?"

**Problem:** Assumes traditional is better, which is a value judgment, not a researchable claim.

**Solution approach:**
1. Identify the assumption
2. Ask: "Is this assumption something you want to test, or accept as given?"
3. Reframe to make assumptions explicit or testable

**Improved:**
> "How do residents rate satisfaction with traditional versus modern residential facades?"

---

## Measurability Issues

### No Observable Outcome

**Symptoms:**
- No way to know when question is answered
- Outcomes are purely conceptual
- Success cannot be measured

**Example:**
> "How does architecture express cultural identity?"

**Problem:** "Express" is not observable. How would you measure expression?

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "What would you observe to answer this question?"
2. Ask: "What data could you collect?"
3. Help operationalize abstract concepts

**Improved:**
> "How do residents describe the cultural significance of architectural elements in their neighborhood?"

---

### Unmeasurable Variables

**Symptoms:**
- Key concepts cannot be quantified or observed
- No established measurement methods
- Variables are too abstract

**Example:**
> "How does urban design affect happiness?"

**Problem:** "Happiness" is difficult to measure reliably, and "urban design" is too broad to isolate.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "What indicator of happiness could you measure?"
2. Suggest established measures (satisfaction surveys, behavioral indicators)
3. Narrow the scope of both variables

**Improved:**
> "How does access to green space within 500m of residence correlate with self-reported life satisfaction?"

---

## Context Issues

### Missing Population

**Symptoms:**
- Unclear who/what is being studied
- Findings couldn't generalize because population undefined
- "People" or "users" without specification

**Example:**
> "How do people navigate complex buildings?"

**Problem:** Which people? First-time visitors? Regular users? People with disabilities?

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "Who specifically are you interested in?"
2. Ask: "Why this group?"
3. Help define inclusion/exclusion criteria

**Improved:**
> "How do first-time visitors navigate wayfinding in hospital complexes?"

---

### Missing Setting

**Symptoms:**
- No geographic or contextual boundaries
- Unclear where research applies
- Results couldn't be replicated

**Example:**
> "How effective are green roofs at reducing stormwater runoff?"

**Problem:** Effectiveness depends heavily on climate, building type, and implementation.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "In what climate or region?"
2. Ask: "On what type of buildings?"
3. Help bound the study appropriately

**Improved:**
> "How effective are extensive green roofs at reducing stormwater runoff on commercial buildings in temperate maritime climates?"

---

### Missing Timeframe

**Symptoms:**
- Unclear when the study takes place
- Time-sensitive phenomena without temporal bounds
- Historical vs. contemporary unclear

**Example:**
> "How is public space used in Weimar?"

**Problem:** Usage patterns change by season, day, time. Historical or current use?

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "What time period are you interested in?"
2. Ask: "Do you want to study change over time, or a snapshot?"
3. Help define temporal boundaries

**Improved:**
> "How do patterns of public space use in Weimar's Theaterplatz differ between summer weekends and winter weekends?"

---

## Structural Issues

### Question Disguised as Statement

**Symptoms:**
- Written as a declaration, not a question
- No interrogative structure
- Conclusion already embedded

**Example:**
> "This research examines how simulation improves design outcomes."

**Problem:** This assumes simulation improves outcomes. It's a statement, not a question.

**Solution approach:**
1. Convert to genuine question form
2. Remove embedded assumptions
3. Make the inquiry open to multiple findings

**Improved:**
> "Does using simulation during early design phases affect the energy efficiency of final building designs?"

---

### Method Embedded in Question

**Symptoms:**
- Question specifies the method
- Method drives the question instead of vice versa
- Limits possible approaches unnecessarily

**Example:**
> "How can machine learning be used to analyze urban patterns?"

**Problem:** This assumes ML is the right approach. Maybe simpler methods work better.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "What do you actually want to know about urban patterns?"
2. Separate the phenomenon from the method
3. Let the question drive method selection

**Improved:**
> "What spatial patterns characterize high pedestrian activity areas in historic city centers?"

(Method becomes a methodological choice, not part of the question)

---

## Computational Design Specific Issues

### Model-Driven vs. Data-Driven Confusion

**Symptoms:**
- Unclear whether the study tests a model or explores data
- Mixed objectives (prediction vs. understanding)
- Conflating model performance with domain insights

**Example:**
> "How can neural networks understand building layouts?"

**Problem:** Neural networks don't "understand" - they predict. This conflates AI capability with human understanding.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "Are you testing if ML can predict something, or trying to understand layouts yourself?"
2. Clarify the research objective
3. Separate technical capability from domain knowledge

**Improved (if prediction):**
> "How accurately can a convolutional neural network predict occupancy flow from floor plan images?"

**Improved (if understanding):**
> "What spatial features of floor plans correlate with efficient occupancy flow?"

---

### Overfitting to Training Data

**Symptoms:**
- No mention of test/validation data
- Question assumes model will "work" without validation
- No consideration of generalization

**Example:**
> "How can we train a GAN to generate floor plans?"

**Problem:** Training a model is not research - evaluating whether it generalizes is.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "How will you know if the generated floor plans are any good?"
2. Ask: "Will the model work on building types not in the training set?"
3. Shift focus from training to evaluation

**Improved:**
> "How does a GAN trained on German residential floor plans perform when generating layouts for French housing standards?"

---

### Missing Ground Truth for Evaluation

**Symptoms:**
- No definition of what "good" output means
- Success criteria undefined
- Evaluation deferred or assumed

**Example:**
> "Can AI generate creative building designs?"

**Problem:** "Creative" has no objective measure. Who judges creativity? By what criteria?

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "How would you recognize a 'creative' design?"
2. Ask: "Who will evaluate the outputs?"
3. Help define measurable evaluation criteria

**Improved:**
> "How do architecture students rate the novelty of AI-generated facade designs compared to student-generated designs using a validated creativity assessment rubric?"

---

### Simulation vs. Reality Gap

**Symptoms:**
- Simulation results assumed to transfer to reality
- No validation against real-world data
- Gap between model and reality not addressed

**Example:**
> "How do pedestrians behave according to our simulation?"

**Problem:** The question asks about the simulation, not about real pedestrians. Simulation behavior isn't inherently interesting.

**Solution approach:**
1. Ask: "What real-world behavior are you trying to predict or explain?"
2. Ask: "How will you validate the simulation against reality?"
3. Reframe to focus on predictive accuracy or insight generation

**Improved:**
> "How accurately does agent-based simulation predict pedestrian route choice in train station concourses compared to observed tracking data?"

---

## Quick Diagnosis Checklist

| Check | Question to Ask |
|-------|-----------------|
| Can I identify exactly what will be measured? | If no → measurability issue |
| Can I identify who/what is being studied? | If no → missing population |
| Can I identify where this takes place? | If no → missing setting |
| Is this one question or several? | If several → bundled questions |
| Could this be answered in a paragraph? | If yes → too narrow |
| Could this be a whole book? | If yes → too broad |
| Are all key terms clearly defined? | If no → clarity issue |
| Does this assume something unproven? | If yes → hidden assumption |
| Is there a ground truth for evaluation? | If no (ML/AI) → missing ground truth |
| Is generalization addressed? | If no (ML/AI) → overfitting concern |
| Is the simulation validated? | If no (simulation) → reality gap |
