# Example: Design Concept File

This is a real `_design_concept.md` from the thesis-topics project. It demonstrates how to define a consistent visual identity across all images in a project.

# Image Design Concept — Thesis Topics

## Visual Identity

All topic images share a single visual language: **miniature architectural models photographed as physical objects on a studio surface.** The images look like someone hand-cast small urban scenes from clay and concrete, arranged them on a white table, and photographed them from above. They are not renders, not diagrams, not illustrations — they feel tactile, real, and crafted.

The style is inspired by the reference image (`style_reference.png`): cast terracotta houses with visible air bubbles, rough edges, and warm directional light against a clean white background. The models look like they were made in a workshop — imperfect, solid, and honest.

## Camera

- **Isometric view from above**, camera at roughly 45° looking diagonally across the arrangement.
- Consistent across all topics — the viewer always looks down at the models from the same angle.
- The entire arrangement must fit within the frame with white margin on all sides. No cropping.

## Material Palette (two materials only)

| Material | Look | Meaning |
|----------|------|---------|
| **Terracotta** | Warm reddish-brown, matte, with visible pores and air bubbles. Same material as the style reference. | The **human, permanent, or designed** layer — buildings, established urban fabric, intentional design, community, the things people care about. |
| **Grey concrete** | Cool grey, matte, same tactile quality as the terracotta but clearly different in color. | The **systemic, changing, or problematic** layer — infrastructure, vehicles, AI-generated output, temporary elements, the things that shift or need attention. |

The two-material contrast is the core visual device. Every topic uses it to encode its central tension.

## Composition

- **Square tiles** are the base unit. Each scene is built on one or more flat square tiles (like coasters or plinths), arranged on the white surface.
- What varies per topic:
  - **Number and arrangement** of tiles (single tile, 2x2 grid, row of three, cluster, split tile)
  - **What sits on each tile** (a street segment, a plaza, a neighborhood fragment)
  - **How terracotta and grey are distributed** across and within tiles
- Tiles can be elevated on pedestals/columns of varying heights to suggest measurement or comparison.

## Detail Level

- Buildings are simplified blocks with carved windows and doors — recognizable as buildings but clearly miniature and abstract.
- Trees are either carved from the same material or made from real dried twigs/branches placed into the model.
- Human figures are tiny, abstract, and made of the same concrete/terracotta material — not realistic.
- Street elements (benches, cars, bike lanes, market stalls) are recognizable but miniature and simplified.

## What to Avoid

- No text, labels, annotations, or UI elements in the image.
- No realistic people or photographic elements.
- No digital/screen aesthetics — no glowing, no wireframes, no overlays.
- No walls, ceilings, or room boundaries — just the white surface extending to the edges.
- No gradients, fog, or atmospheric effects — sharp and clear edge to edge.
- No color beyond the terracotta/grey palette (exception: occasional natural elements like pink blossoms on twig trees, green moss — used sparingly for a specific narrative purpose).
